**Menstrual Health Awareness Game: Health Promotion & Education**

Menstrual health is an integral part of the overall health of a person, though it is always associated with stigma, misinformation, and lack of resources. Various problems, such as menstrual pain, infections, irregular cycles, and poor management of menstrual hygiene, can affect health and quality of life. Many of these challenges are preventable or can be managed with proper education on the subject, access to hygiene products, and supportive practices. This will, in turn, enable people and communities to make informed decisions and act towards better menstrual health; towards improving health and gender equity across communities; as enshrined by the global UN effort, popularly called the Sustainable Development Goals - SDGs or 'the 2030 agenda'. Specifically, for this educational game, SDG goal 3 (good health) and SDG 5 (gender equality) apply cogently.

The game is designed to increase awareness and improve knowledge on menstrual health and ways of managing its challenges. It is based on public health models such as iCCM, operant conditioning principles, both positive and negative reinforcement, and social theories including social cognitive theory and the socio-ecological model. The game is not for girls alone, but for boys too, and indeed, the entire family as well.

We hope this game will help people become more aware and take action for menstrual health, leading to healthier and more inclusive communities.

### GAME RULES

- **Players**:  
   The game can be played by **2 to 4 players** at a time. Each player has a counter (token) of a different color (red, yellow, green, or blue).
- **Starting the Game**:  
   To start the game, you must roll a **'six'** on the dice. If you roll any other number, your turn is skipped. After you roll a six, you move your counter forward based on the number you roll on subsequent turns.
- **Movement**:
  - If you land on the foot of a **ladder**, you get to **climb** and move ahead; ONCE YOU ANSWER A SELECTED QUESTION FROM THE SECTION B PART OF THE BOOK CORRECTLY (you get a boost).
  - If you land on a **snake** head, you get **sent backward (you get a dip)**. BUT IF YOU COULD ANSWER A QUESTION CORRECTLY FROM THE SECTION B PART OF THIS BOOKLET AT THESE POINTS OF POTENTIAL FALL, YOU GET TO FORFEIT THE DIP, SO YOU STAY WHERE YOU ARE AND CONTINUE WITH YOUR JOURNEY.
  - Some spaces are **neutral**, meaning nothing happens (no reward or punishment).
  - If you land on a **picture with a question mark, you have to answer the question in this script/ question cards. (Section A questions)**
- **Rewards and Punishments**:
  - **Rewards**: If you land on a space that represents good menstrual health practices (e.g., using hygienic menstrual products, eating a balanced diet, or seeking medical advice for irregular cycles), you will receive a reward (e.g., climb a ladder or move up an arrow). BUT you can only earn those rewards by answering questions correctly, which allows you to climb.
  - **Punishments**:If you land on a space that represents risky menstrual health behaviors (e.g., neglecting hygiene, using unsafe products, or believing myths about menstruation), you will face a punishment (e.g., move backward due to a snake or an arrow pointing down). If you answer a question correctly in these spaces, however, you avoid the punishment and stay where you are.
- **Answering Questions**:
  - Players have to answer the corresponding question number if they land on a picture with a question mark in Section A.
  - Players can choose any question (1 to 400) from the game Q and A's in Section B to answer, whether it's a **True/False** question or a regular **Q&A** type question. The moderator or facilitator reads the chosen question and all players and the facilitator judge the correctness or otherwise of the answer given.
- OPTIONAL: there is ONE chance for a change of question. But the moderator must always clarify answers/provide the correct answer for unanswered or unsatisfactorily answered questions (from the resource in the book).
- **Who Can Play**:  
   Both males and females can play the game, as understanding menstrual health and reducing stigma is important for everyone in building supportive communities.
- **Winning the Game**:
  - Players are encouraged to develop their own strategies to finish the game faster and win, but they must follow the rules and play fairly.

The game will help players enhance their understanding of menstrual health, develop positive attitudes and behaviors towards menstrual hygiene management, and have fun while learning.

**DYSMENORRHOEA, SECONDARY DYSMENORRHOEA, PID, FIBROIDS, ADENOMYOSIS, ENDOMETRIOSIS AND NEUROMODULATION**

**IMPORTANT DEFINITIONS**

**Dysmenorrhoea**

Dysmenorrhoea refers to painful menstruation characterized by cramping lower abdominal pain occurring before or during menstruation.

**Primary Dysmenorrhoea**

Primary dysmenorrhoea is painful menstruation occurring in the absence of identifiable pelvic pathology. It is largely related to excess production of uterine prostaglandins.

**Secondary Dysmenorrhoea**

Secondary dysmenorrhoea is painful menstruation resulting from an underlying pelvic pathology such as endometriosis, adenomyosis, pelvic inflammatory disease, or uterine fibroids.

**Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)**

PID is an infection and inflammation of the upper female reproductive tract involving the uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries, and surrounding pelvic structures.

**Uterine Fibroids (Leiomyomas)**

Fibroids are benign smooth muscle tumors of the uterus.

**Adenomyosis**

Adenomyosis occurs when endometrial glands and stroma are found within the uterine muscle (myometrium).

**Endometriosis**

Endometriosis is the presence of endometrial-like tissue outside the uterine cavity.

**Neuromodulation**

Neuromodulation refers to therapeutic alteration of nerve activity using electrical, magnetic, or chemical stimulation.

**TENS**

Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS) is a non-invasive therapy that delivers electrical impulses through the skin to reduce pain.

**Interferential Therapy (IFT)**

A neuromodulation technique using intersecting medium-frequency currents to reduce pain and improve circulation.

**Sacral Neuromodulation**

Electrical stimulation of sacral nerves to modify pelvic pain and pelvic organ dysfunction.

**Expanded Definitions**

**Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)**

**Definition:**  
Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) is an ascending infection of the upper female genital tract involving the endometrium (endometritis), fallopian tubes (salpingitis), ovaries (oophoritis), pelvic peritoneum (pelvic peritonitis), or tubo-ovarian complex. It is most commonly caused by sexually transmitted organisms such as _Chlamydia trachomatis_ and _Neisseria gonorrhoeae_, although polymicrobial infections involving anaerobes, facultative bacteria, and genital mycoplasmas are common.

**Clinical significance:**  
PID is a major cause of secondary dysmenorrhoea, chronic pelvic pain, infertility, ectopic pregnancy, and tubo-ovarian abscess formation.

**Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS)**

**Definition:**  
Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS) is a non-invasive neuromodulation technique that uses low-voltage electrical currents delivered through electrodes placed on the skin to stimulate peripheral nerves and reduce pain perception.

**Mechanisms of action:**

- **Gate Control Theory**
  - Activation of large-diameter A-beta sensory fibers inhibits transmission of pain signals carried by smaller A-delta and C fibers at the spinal cord level.
- **Endogenous Opioid Release**
  - Stimulates release of endorphins, enkephalins, and dynorphins.
- **Central Pain Modulation**
  - Alters pain processing within the spinal cord and brain.
- **Improved Blood Flow**
  - May improve local circulation and reduce ischemic pain.

**Clinical uses**

- Primary dysmenorrhoea
- Chronic pelvic pain
- Musculoskeletal pain
- Labour pain
- Post-operative pain

**Evidence Base**  
Systematic reviews and randomized controlled trials support high-frequency TENS as an effective non-pharmacological treatment for primary dysmenorrhoea, particularly when NSAIDs are ineffective, contraindicated, or poorly tolerated.

**PRIMARY DYSMENORRHOEA**

**Key Definition**

**Primary Dysmenorrhoea**

Primary dysmenorrhoea is painful menstruation occurring in the absence of identifiable pelvic pathology. It is thought to result primarily from excessive production of uterine prostaglandins, leading to increased uterine contractions, uterine ischemia, and pain.

**Question 101**

A 16-year-old girl presents with crampy lower abdominal pain that begins a few hours before menstruation and lasts for 24-48 hours. Pelvic examination and ultrasonography are normal. The most likely diagnosis is:

**Answer:** Primary dysmenorrhoea

**Explanation:** The hallmark of primary dysmenorrhoea is cyclical menstrual pain occurring without identifiable pelvic pathology, typically beginning within a few years after menarche.

**Question 102**

**Explanation:** Elevated prostaglandin F2α causes uterine hypercontractility, vasoconstriction, ischemia, and pain.

**EVIDENCE-BASED MANAGEMENT OF DYSMENORRHOEA: NEUROMODULATION, TENS, AND NON-PHARMACOLOGICAL THERAPIES**

**Key Definitions**

**Neuromodulation**

Neuromodulation is the therapeutic alteration of nerve activity through targeted delivery of electrical, magnetic, or chemical stimuli to specific neurological sites.

**TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)**

TENS is a non-invasive method of pain control that delivers electrical impulses through electrodes placed on the skin to modulate pain perception.

**PENS (Percutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)**

PENS combines principles of acupuncture and electrical stimulation by delivering electrical currents through fine needles inserted into tissues.

**Interferential Therapy (IFT)**

A form of electrical stimulation using two medium-frequency currents that intersect within tissues to produce therapeutic effects.

**Sacral Neuromodulation (SNM)**

Electrical stimulation of sacral nerve roots, typically S3, to alter pelvic organ and pain pathways.
